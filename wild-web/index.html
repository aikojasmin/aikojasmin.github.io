In Loving Memory of

Adobe Flash Player

1996 _ 2020







The life and death of Adobe Flash
Let’s pretend we’re back in, say, 2008. You’ve just bought a new computer with Windows XP on it, hooked it up to the internet, opened the browser, navigated to your favorite website… and found that half of it doesn’t display. “Try installing Adobe Flash,” a friend who knows all about computers advises over the phone.

The origins of Flash date back to the early 1990s: it was a tool to create simple vector-based animations compact enough to be downloaded even over a slow internet connection via a modem.

By the late 2000s, Adobe Flash Player — still an animation tool at heart — was pretty much indispensable. Without it, literally half of all websites didn’t work.

At the same time, cybercriminals began to utilize the dozens upon dozens of vulnerabilities found in the player. Largely because of this, since 2010, Flash has had many ardent critics, and even Adobe itself recognized that the internet needs to develop along different lines.

Nevertheless, Flash’s “funeral” was drawn out for almost 10 years — and even then it wasn’t properly buried. It all makes for one of the most interesting episodes in the history of online information security.

Some say Steve Jobs killed Adobe Flash Player, others blame the inevitability of change. I say, it doesn't matter who did it! It's gone now. And it's never coming back. So, everyone take good hard look at themselves in the mirror and remember that life is short. Kings can fall from the highest of heights. If there's one thing Adobe Flash Player would have wanted us to remember, it would be that creativity cannot be controlled, rather it should be nurtured. Long live Adobe Flash player. Forever in our hearts.

Here Lies


MSN
Messenger

2000 _ 2010







It was the late 90s and social media platforms like MySpace and Facebook didn't yet exist. Texting was still in its infancy as very few people actually owned mobile phones at that point. E-mail was a popular method of communication among those with access to a computer, but it lacked the real-time feel that makes in-person conversation so appealing.

For that, you needed an instant messaging program, and when the mainstream Internet movement really started to take root, there were four major competitors jockeying for position: AOL Instant Messenger, ICQ, Yahoo! Messenger and Microsoft's MSN Messenger.

MSN Messenger came out on top as every 12 year old's first choice as a means to gossip and chat to their crushes after school. From static emoticons, to later animated emoticons of large lips laughing in the middle of your screen - reminicent of a Howler from Harry Potter; It could be said that MSN paved the way for Facebook Messenger, and other apps alike.

Sadly, things went from bad to worse in 2012 when Microsoft forced Windows Vista and Windows 7 users to upgrade from an older version of the app to a newer release, and dropped support for Windows XP entirely shortly after. This was around the same time Microsoft acquired Skype.

On a dark day in 2013, we said farewell to MSN Messenger through teary eyes. With leaving only the memory of its homepage and chat boxes to occupy the darkest, deepest crevices of our minds. I owe my fast typing to MSN Messenger. Rest in eternal peace. Gone, but never forgotten.

Dedicated to the memory of

Limewire

2000 _ 2010










Dubbed as a site that nearly destroyed the music industry in the early 2000s, Limewire stands tall as a beacon to amateur music piracy enthusiasts. If it weren't for the many bugs and viruses Limewire sadly contracted, and the eminent destruction it posed to the music industry, it would be alive today.

At its peak, there were several million people using the service to collect all the music they wanted, entirely for free, and while it’s impossible to nail down exactly how much all of that collective stealing could have been worth, there were several accounts that claimed it was somewhere between billions of dollars and trillions, though that latter suggestion is difficult to substantiate. Iconic.

While Limewire was ousted of existence almost immediately, some people just can't seem to let the dead rest peacefully. There have been many attempts to resurrect the platform, but all have never made it to see the light of day. Here's to hoping Limewire can finally rest easy.

Cherished Always

Club Penguin

2007 _ 2014









After accumulating hundreds of millions of users over its 11 years, the party was finally over. Club Penguin would shutdown. The owners of Club Penguin gave users until the end of March to get their affairs and igloos in order. For the younger generation, the shutdown was the end of an era.

Like Neopets and Habbo Hotel, the site helped show digital-natives the impact that virtual worlds can have on our own. Club Penguin created its own ecosystem of inside jokes, traditions, and memes, at a time where the latter was mostly limited photos of an animal and two lines of Impact font. After Donald Trump won the presidential election in 2016, protests broke out on the island, with penguins letting out text-based chants of "not my president" and "my body my choice" on the snow-covered streets.

In the case of Club Penguins death, it seems there must be only one suspect: MONEY. The pivot to cater to a relevant game-consuming landscape offered a better way to cash in, as in-game purchases and more frequent advertising are a standard of the industry. This clashed with Club Penguin's original mission; to serve as a space on the internet for kids to play and interact safely, free from ads. Today, there are virtually no online platforms designed for children that don't rely on sponsored content in some form. After being purchased by the Walt Disney Company for $700 million, that became true for Club Penguin as well.

Now, Club Penguin is dead. A once thriving hub for kids to safely socialise and play is gone. Remnants of the online platform remain only as screen recordings on YouTube. Long live Club Penguin. Life will never be the same without you.

In Eternal Peace

Vine

2013 _ 2017









The thing about Vine becoming the internet’s premier tool for making short-form videos is that it happened almost completely by accident. Its founders had envisioned their tool for making 6_second clips as a way to help people capture casual moments in their lives and share them with friends.

This platform eventuated into a global sensation _ a platform that connected the famous with the general public in a way that made them feel like everyday people. A short-form video platform made to capture casual moments in the lives of its users became a window into the lives of the rich and famous, and the breeding ground for iconic memes, and iconic moments in pop culture that live on today.

Now, it may seem like Vine is a distant memory, a fleeting moment in time, a blip in the Matrix. A short life, but a good one. I believe their last words were "here for a good time, not a long time".
